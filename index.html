Business Context & Problem Statement
Research Nester Analytics operated a high-volume research consulting model ‚Äî delivering 12‚Äì13 projects per week across multiple analysts, clients, and verticals. As the team scaled, the absence of a structured workflow management system became a critical operational liability. Projects were tracked through a combination of email threads, WhatsApp messages, individual spreadsheets, and verbal status updates.

The result: project managers had no reliable picture of where any given project stood at any time. Deadlines were missed reactively rather than prevented proactively. Workload was distributed informally ‚Äî some analysts were overloaded while others had spare capacity. There was no quality control checkpoint before delivery, and no performance data to learn from. The entire system depended on individual memory and goodwill, which broke down under volume.
Root Cause Analysis ‚Äî 5-Why Framework
Why were delivery timelines being missed?
Why 1Project managers didn't know which projects were at risk until it was too late.
Why 2There was no real-time tracking system ‚Äî status relied on analysts proactively updating scattered spreadsheets.
Why 3There was no standardized intake process, so projects entered the system inconsistently with missing information.
Why 4No centralized request intake meant each project had a different paper trail across different communication channels.
Why 5 (Root Cause)The workflow was never formally designed ‚Äî it evolved organically as the team grew, without structured documentation, ownership, or tooling.

BA Contributions ‚Äî What I Did
  • Stakeholder Requirement GatheringConducted structured discussions with project managers, research analysts, and leadership to elicit current pain points, desired outcomes, and success criteria. Mapped each stakeholder's "need vs. want" to separate core requirements from nice-to-haves.

  • TO-BE Solution DesignDesigned a 5-stage workflow with a centralized intake system, real-time tracking, capacity management, QC gate, and automated alerting ‚Äî specifying functional requirements for each stage.
  • KPI Framework & Dashboard RequirementsDefined 5 KPIs with clear calculation logic, data sources, and target thresholds. Wrote Power BI dashboard requirements specifying what each stakeholder tier needed to see and why.

Business Context & Problem Statement
As Research Nester Analytics scaled, leadership required consistent, reliable performance reporting to make resourcing, pricing, and strategic decisions. The reality was far from this: reporting was entirely manual. Analysts spent 3‚Äì5 hours each week pulling data from multiple spreadsheets, reformatting it into decks, and sending individual summaries to different managers ‚Äî each of whom had slightly different versions of the same metrics.

Critically, there was no agreed-upon KPI definition. "Turnaround time" meant different things in different reports. "Project completion" was counted differently by different teams. Leadership was making decisions based on inconsistent, potentially contradictory data. There was no dashboard they could trust, no single view they could point to, and no way to track trends over time. The organisation was data-rich but insight-poor.
Root Cause Analysis ‚Äî Reporting Failure Tree
Why was leadership lacking reliable decision-support data?
Why 1Reports presented to leadership were inconsistent and often contradicted each other.
Why 2Different teams defined the same KPIs differently ‚Äî no unified data dictionary or metric definitions.
Why 3There was no standardized reporting template ‚Äî each person built their own format from scratch each week.
Why 4Reporting was a manual, ad-hoc task ‚Äî no automation, no shared data source, no refresh schedule.
Why 5 (Root Cause)No one had ever formally owned "reporting" as a business process. It grew as an afterthought of operations, never as a designed system with owners, standards, or tooling.

BA Contributions ‚Äî What I Did
  • Reporting Inefficiency AuditSystematically mapped all existing reporting activities ‚Äî who was producing what, for whom, how often, from which data sources. Quantified time spent: ~25 analyst-hours per week across the team on reporting that was partially duplicated and inconsistently formatted.
  • KPI Elicitation WorkshopsFacilitated structured sessions with each stakeholder tier to define the metrics that actually drove decisions. Challenged stakeholders to articulate "what action would you take if this number changed?" ‚Äî to filter out vanity metrics and retain decision-driving KPIs.
  • Unified KPI Data DictionaryDocumented agreed definitions for every metric ‚Äî calculation logic, data source, refresh frequency, owner, and acceptable threshold. This single document became the reference standard to eliminate metric inconsistency across the organization.
  • Dashboard Requirements DocumentationWrote formal dashboard requirements for each stakeholder tier ‚Äî specifying data fields, visualization types, filter options, refresh schedules, and access permissions. Translated business questions ("are we on track?") into data requirements ("show rolling 4-week TAT vs. 8-hour target").
  • Tiered Template StandardizationDesigned three standardized reporting templates (daily operational, weekly management, monthly executive) with fixed structure, agreed KPIs, and color-coded threshold indicators. Eliminated the version conflict problem entirely.
  • Power BI Dashboard Design & SpecificationProduced detailed wireframes and functional specifications for each Power BI dashboard ‚Äî specifying page layout, chart types, interactivity, drill-through logic, and conditional formatting rules for threshold alerts.

Reflection

Key Learnings

What these engagements taught me about effective Business Analysis ‚Äî lessons that go beyond methodology and into the mindset of good BA practice.

üí°
Visibility Reduces Friction
When teams can see project status in real-time, communication shifts from reactive to proactive. Transparency is a product feature ‚Äî designing it in intentionally saves dozens of status-check conversations every week.
üéØ
Standardization Creates Shared Language
Inconsistent definitions are not just inefficient ‚Äî they erode trust. A unified KPI data dictionary doesn't just save time on reporting; it eliminates the low-level organizational conflict that comes from different teams "winning" their interpretation of a metric.
üìä
KPIs Must Drive Actions
The right question to ask when defining a KPI: "what decision would you make if this number changed?" If there's no clear answer, it's a vanity metric. Good BA means being willing to push back on the metrics stakeholders think they want.
üë•
Alignment Early Saves Rework Late
Stakeholder misalignment is the #1 cause of delivery failure. Running a proper elicitation session in week 1 ‚Äî even when it feels slow ‚Äî prevents the far more expensive experience of rebuilding a solution that solves the wrong problem.
üöÄ
Adoption Is the Real Success Metric
A technically excellent solution that nobody uses is a failed BA engagement. Designing for adoption ‚Äî keeping intake forms under 2 minutes, making dashboards load fast, building for how people actually work ‚Äî separates good analysis from great analysis.
üîç
Root Causes Over Symptoms
5-Why analysis revealed that "deadline misses" were not a motivation problem ‚Äî they were a visibility and structure problem. Treating the symptom (reminders, pressure) would have failed. Treating the root cause (no designed workflow) created lasting change.
Guiding Principle
"Focus on solving business problems ‚Äî not just documenting requirements."

Documentation is an output of good analysis ‚Äî not the goal itself. Every requirement, every user story, every process map exists in service of a business outcome that matters to a real person.
