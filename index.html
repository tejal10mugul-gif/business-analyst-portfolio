Business Context & Problem Statement
Research Nester Analytics operated a high-volume research consulting model — delivering 12–13 projects per week across multiple analysts, clients, and verticals. As the team scaled, the absence of a structured workflow management system became a critical operational liability. Projects were tracked through a combination of email threads, WhatsApp messages, individual spreadsheets, and verbal status updates.

The result: project managers had no reliable picture of where any given project stood at any time. Deadlines were missed reactively rather than prevented proactively. Workload was distributed informally — some analysts were overloaded while others had spare capacity. There was no quality control checkpoint before delivery, and no performance data to learn from.
Root Cause Analysis — 5-Why Framework
Why were delivery timelines being missed?
Why 1Project managers didn't know which projects were at risk until it was too late.
Why 2There was no real-time tracking system — status relied on analysts proactively updating scattered spreadsheets.
Why 3There was no standardized intake process, so projects entered the system inconsistently with missing information.
Why 4No centralized request intake meant each project had a different paper trail across different communication channels.
Why 5 (Root Cause)The workflow was never formally designed — it evolved organically as the team grew, without structured documentation, ownership, or tooling.

Business Context & Problem Statement
A mid-sized retail bank offering SME loans (₹5L–₹2Cr) was experiencing a critical operational problem: the average time from loan application to disbursement was 25–30 working days — well above the industry benchmark of 10–14 days. As a result, 34% of applicants were abandoning applications mid-process or switching to faster-moving fintech competitors.

The bank was losing qualified borrowers to fintechs offering 5–7 day approvals, directly impacting fee income, NPA ratios, and SME portfolio growth targets.
Root Cause Analysis — 5-Why Framework
Why was average loan TAT 25–30 days instead of the target 12 days?
Why 1Applications were re-entering processing stages multiple times due to missing or incorrect documents — creating rework loops that added 7–10 days per cycle.
Why 2Applicants were submitting incomplete documentation because no standardized checklist existed at the point of application intake.
Why 3Credit assessment, legal review, and risk scoring ran sequentially — each team waiting for the previous stage's sign-off before beginning their own review.
Why 4There was no shared system across credit, legal, risk, and branch teams — each team worked in their own spreadsheet/email system.
Why 5 (Root Cause)The loan origination process was never formally designed as an end-to-end workflow. It evolved organically across siloed departments — no single owner, no SLA definition, no stage-gate criteria.

Artifact 2 — SQL Data Audit & Diagnostic Queries
Purpose of SQL Audit
Before defining dashboard requirements or KPIs, a BA must first audit the data that will feed them. These queries were designed to answer: Is the data complete? Is it consistent? Is it at the right granularity? The audit findings then directly shaped which KPIs were feasible to define and what data engineering work was required before dashboard build.
Query 1 — SKU Sell-Through Velocity by Channel (Weekly)
Business Question: Which SKUs are selling fastest per channel per week? Used to rank SKUs by velocity for the decision matrix.
SELECT
  s.sku_code,
  m.sku_name,
  m.category,
  m.pack_size,
  s.channel,  -- 'modern_trade' | 'general_trade' | 'ecommerce'
  s.week_ending,
  SUM(s.units_sold) AS weekly_units,
  SUM(s.value_inr) AS weekly_revenue,
  ROUND(AVG(s.units_sold) OVER (
    PARTITION BY s.sku_code, s.channel
    ORDER BY s.week_ending
    ROWS BETWEEN 3 PRECEDING AND CURRENT ROW
  ), 1) AS rolling_4wk_avg_units
FROM sell_through s
JOIN sku_master m ON s.sku_code = m.sku_code
WHERE s.week_ending >= DATEADD(week, -12, GETDATE())
  AND s.channel IN ('modern_trade', 'general_trade', 'ecommerce')
GROUP BY s.sku_code, m.sku_name, m.category, m.pack_size, s.channel, s.week_ending
ORDER BY weekly_revenue DESC;
→Audit Finding: Query revealed SKU code mismatch between sell_through table (ERP codes) and sku_master (internal codes) — 23% of records had no join match. Data engineering task required: build cross-reference mapping table before dashboard can be built.
Query 2 — Distributor Fill Rate by SKU × Geography
Business Question: For each SKU, what % of distributor orders are fulfilled on time and in full (OTIF)? Low fill rate = supply constraint, not weak demand — critical to separate these before expansion decision.
SELECT
  d.sku_code,
  d.distributor_id,
  g.state,
  g.region_tier,  -- 'metro' | 'tier1' | 'tier2'
  COUNT(d.order_id) AS total_orders,
  SUM(CASE WHEN d.qty_delivered >= d.qty_ordered THEN 1 ELSE 0 END) AS full_fill_orders,
  ROUND(
    100.0 * SUM(CASE WHEN d.qty_delivered >= d.qty_ordered THEN 1 ELSE 0 END)
    / NULLIF(COUNT(d.order_id), 0)
  , 1) AS fill_rate_pct,
  COUNT(CASE WHEN d.qty_delivered = 0 THEN 1 END) AS stockout_count
FROM distributor_orders d
JOIN geography_master g ON d.distributor_id = g.distributor_id
WHERE d.order_date >= DATEADD(month, -3, GETDATE())
GROUP BY d.sku_code, d.distributor_id, g.state, g.region_tier
HAVING COUNT(d.order_id) >= 5  -- exclude distributors with too few orders for reliable rate
ORDER BY fill_rate_pct ASC;
→Audit Finding: 3 high-volume SKUs had fill rates below 60% in Tier-2 geographies — flagged as supply-constrained, not demand-constrained. Expansion decision for these SKUs requires supply chain fix before category push. This finding changed the shortlist composition entirely.
Query 3 — E-Commerce Pin Code Demand Heatmap (Latent Demand Signal)
Business Question: Where are customers ordering online from cities/pin codes with no or weak offline distribution? High online order volume + low offline penetration = latent demand signal for expansion priority geography.
SELECT
  e.sku_code,
  e.delivery_pincode,
  p.city,
  p.state,
  SUM(e.units_ordered) AS online_units_3m,
  COUNT(DISTINCT e.customer_id) AS unique_buyers,
  COALESCE(od.offline_units_3m, 0) AS offline_units_3m,
  CASE
    WHEN COALESCE(od.offline_units_3m, 0) = 0 THEN 'No Offline Presence'
    WHEN e.units_ordered > od.offline_units_3m * 2 THEN 'High Latent Demand'
    ELSE 'Balanced'
  END AS demand_signal
FROM ecom_orders e
JOIN pincode_master p ON e.delivery_pincode = p.pincode
LEFT JOIN (
  SELECT sku_code, distributor_pinzone, SUM(units_sold) AS offline_units_3m
  FROM general_trade_sales
  WHERE sale_date >= DATEADD(month, -3, GETDATE())
  GROUP BY sku_code, distributor_pinzone
) od ON e.sku_code = od.sku_code AND p.pinzone = od.distributor_pinzone
WHERE e.order_date >= DATEADD(month, -3, GETDATE())
GROUP BY e.sku_code, e.delivery_pincode, p.city, p.state, od.offline_units_3m
HAVING SUM(e.units_ordered) >= 50  -- minimum signal threshold
ORDER BY online_units_3m DESC;
→Audit Finding: 8 pin codes in Tier-2 cities (Nashik, Coimbatore, Lucknow) showed 'High Latent Demand' for 2 specific SKUs — e-commerce orders with zero offline distribution. These became the priority geography recommendations in the expansion decision matrix.

Artifact 7 — Risk Register
Data Quality23% SKU code mismatch between sell-through and ERP found in SQL audit — dashboard will show incomplete or incorrect data if not resolved. Mitigation: Data engineering team builds cross-reference mapping table as Phase 0 prerequisite; dashboard goes live only after mapping validated by Category Manager and IT. Launch gate: ≥95% SKU match rate required before release.
Stakeholder MisalignmentSales Head may resist shortlist that contradicts sales team's verbal recommendations — risk of the decision matrix being rejected as "not reflecting ground reality." Mitigation: Sales team input included as a qualitative layer in the review process; data-driven shortlist presented as a starting point for discussion, not a mandate. Alignment session held before final shortlist is shared with leadership.
Margin Data AccessFinance team may not grant SKU-level margin data access — without it, the decision matrix loses its most valuable criterion. Mitigation: Formal data access request submitted in Week 1 with use case, data governance scope, and access restriction plan. Escalation path: Category Manager formally requests access at CFO level if blocked at analyst level. Fallback: use product family margin as proxy — document this assumption explicitly in dashboard.
Scope CreepMultiple teams may request additional dashboard pages or KPIs beyond the agreed scope once they see the initial build — delaying the 45-day delivery target. Mitigation: Requirements locked and signed off before build begins; all additional requests logged as Phase 2 backlog items; 45-day launch scope protected. Change control: any scope addition requires Category Manager + IT sign-off and published delay impact.
Distributor Data LatencyGeneral trade distributor data currently arrives monthly as PDFs — weekly dashboard cadence requires weekly structured data feed, which distributors may not be able to provide. Mitigation: In the interim, use biweekly fill rate data with clear "data as of [date]" timestamp on dashboard. Distributor portal rollout (structured data intake) included in Phase 2 roadmap — not a Phase 1 blocker.

Reflection

Key Learnings

What these engagements taught me about effective Business Analysis — lessons that go beyond methodology and into the mindset of good BA practice.

💡
Visibility Reduces Friction
When teams can see project status in real-time, communication shifts from reactive to proactive. Transparency is a product feature — designing it in intentionally saves dozens of status-check conversations every week.
🎯
Standardization Creates Shared Language
Inconsistent definitions are not just inefficient — they erode trust. A unified KPI data dictionary doesn't just save time on reporting; it eliminates the low-level organizational conflict that comes from different teams "winning" their interpretation of a metric.
📊
KPIs Must Drive Actions
The right question to ask when defining a KPI: "what decision would you make if this number changed?" If there's no clear answer, it's a vanity metric. Good BA means being willing to push back on the metrics stakeholders think they want.
👥
Alignment Early Saves Rework Late
Stakeholder misalignment is the #1 cause of delivery failure. Running a proper elicitation session in week 1 — even when it feels slow — prevents the far more expensive experience of rebuilding a solution that solves the wrong problem.
🚀
Adoption Is the Real Success Metric
A technically excellent solution that nobody uses is a failed BA engagement. Designing for adoption — keeping intake forms under 2 minutes, making dashboards load fast, building for how people actually work — separates good analysis from great analysis.
🔍
Root Causes Over Symptoms
5-Why analysis revealed that "deadline misses" were not a motivation problem — they were a visibility and structure problem. Treating the symptom would have failed. Treating the root cause created lasting change.
Guiding Principle
"Focus on solving business problems — not just documenting requirements."

Documentation is an output of good analysis — not the goal itself. Every requirement, every user story, every process map exists in service of a business outcome that matters to a real person.
