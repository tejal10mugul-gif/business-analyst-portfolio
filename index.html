Business Context & Problem Statement
Research Nester Analytics operated a high-volume research consulting model — delivering 12–13 projects per week across multiple analysts, clients, and verticals. As the team scaled, the absence of a structured workflow management system became a critical operational liability. Projects were tracked through a combination of email threads, WhatsApp messages, individual spreadsheets, and verbal status updates.

The result: project managers had no reliable picture of where any given project stood at any time. Deadlines were missed reactively rather than prevented proactively. Workload was distributed informally — some analysts were overloaded while others had spare capacity. There was no quality control checkpoint before delivery, and no performance data to learn from. The entire system depended on individual memory and goodwill, which broke down under volume.
Root Cause Analysis — 5-Why Framework
Why were delivery timelines being missed?
Why 1Project managers didn't know which projects were at risk until it was too late.
Why 2There was no real-time tracking system — status relied on analysts proactively updating scattered spreadsheets.
Why 3There was no standardized intake process, so projects entered the system inconsistently with missing information.
Why 4No centralized request intake meant each project had a different paper trail across different communication channels.
Why 5 (Root Cause)The workflow was never formally designed — it evolved organically as the team grew, without structured documentation, ownership, or tooling.

Business Context & Problem Statement
As Research Nester Analytics scaled, leadership required consistent, reliable performance reporting to make resourcing, pricing, and strategic decisions. The reality was far from this: reporting was entirely manual. Analysts spent 3–5 hours each week pulling data from multiple spreadsheets, reformatting it into decks, and sending individual summaries to different managers — each of whom had slightly different versions of the same metrics.

Critically, there was no agreed-upon KPI definition. "Turnaround time" meant different things in different reports. "Project completion" was counted differently by different teams. Leadership was making decisions based on inconsistent, potentially contradictory data. There was no dashboard they could trust, no single view they could point to, and no way to track trends over time. The organisation was data-rich but insight-poor.
Root Cause Analysis — Reporting Failure Tree
Why was leadership lacking reliable decision-support data?
Why 1Reports presented to leadership were inconsistent and often contradicted each other.
Why 2Different teams defined the same KPIs differently — no unified data dictionary or metric definitions.
Why 3There was no standardized reporting template — each person built their own format from scratch each week.
Why 4Reporting was a manual, ad-hoc task — no automation, no shared data source, no refresh schedule.
Why 5 (Root Cause)No one had ever formally owned "reporting" as a business process. It grew as an afterthought of operations, never as a designed system with owners, standards, or tooling.

Business Context & Problem Statement
A mid-sized retail bank offering SME loans (₹5L–₹2Cr) was experiencing a critical operational problem: the average time from loan application to disbursement was 25–30 working days — well above the industry benchmark of 10–14 days. As a result, 34% of applicants were abandoning applications mid-process or switching to faster-moving fintech competitors.

Branch managers were fielding daily escalations from business owners who couldn't access working capital in time. The credit team was buried in document rework due to incomplete submissions. Compliance officers were flagging applications rejected at late stages — after significant processing effort had already been invested. The bank was losing qualified borrowers to fintechs offering 5–7 day approvals, directly impacting fee income, NPA ratios, and SME portfolio growth targets.
Root Cause Analysis — 5-Why Framework
Why was average loan TAT 25–30 days instead of the target 12 days?
Why 1Applications were re-entering processing stages multiple times due to missing or incorrect documents — creating rework loops that added 7–10 days per cycle.
Why 2Applicants were submitting incomplete documentation because no standardized checklist existed at the point of application intake. Different branches had different requirements.
Why 3Credit assessment, legal review, and risk scoring ran sequentially — each team waiting for the previous stage's sign-off before beginning their own review, adding 6–8 days of idle time.
Why 4There was no shared system across credit, legal, risk, and branch teams — each team worked in their own spreadsheet/email system with no shared visibility into application status.
Why 5 (Root Cause)The loan origination process was never formally designed as an end-to-end workflow. It evolved organically across siloed departments — no single owner, no SLA definition, no stage-gate criteria, and no measurement of where time was actually being lost.

FR-01 — CKYC Record Search
API Purpose: Query the CERSAI CKYC registry to check if a KYC record already exists for the applicant before initiating fresh data collection. Reduces duplicate effort and customer friction.
Input Fields: PAN (mandatory); Aadhaar number (optional, masked); Date of Birth; Full Name (for fuzzy match fallback) Validation Rules: PAN must be 10-character alphanumeric (format: ABCDE1234F); DOB must be in DD/MM/YYYY; API call only triggered post-customer consent confirmation Response Handling: FOUND: pre-populate form fields from CKYC record; display masked data to customer for confirmation. NOT FOUND: proceed to full eKYC / document upload flow Exception Scenarios: CKYC registry timeout (>5 sec) → fallback to manual upload; partial match → flag for compliance review; registry unavailable → log downtime, continue with offline intake Retry Logic: Max 3 automated retries on timeout; exponential backoff (2s, 4s, 8s); alert ops team if all 3 fail Security: HTTPS/TLS 1.3 only; API key + OAuth 2.0 token auth; all API calls logged with timestamp, user session ID, and IP Data Masking: Aadhaar number displayed as XXXX-XXXX-XXXX (last 4 visible only); PAN displayed as XXXXX1234F in UI; raw PAN/Aadhaar never persisted in application logs
FR-02 — CKYC Record Upload
API Purpose: Submit newly verified customer KYC data to the CKYC registry (CERSAI) within T+3 business days of account opening, per RBI mandate. Generates and stores CKYC acknowledgement number.
Input Fields: PAN; Aadhaar masked reference; full name; DOB; gender; address (current & permanent); KYC document type and reference number; account type; KYC completion date; bank branch IFSC Validation Rules: All mandatory fields per CERSAI upload format must be populated; CKYC search must have returned NOT FOUND before upload (no duplicate upload); account must be in active/approved state Response Handling: SUCCESS: store CKYC number in customer master (CBS); update KYC status to CKYC-REGISTERED. FAILURE: flag in compliance queue; trigger T+3 countdown alert Exception Scenarios: Upload rejected due to format error → auto-correct and resubmit within 24 hours; registry down → queue upload with SLA timer; validation error → route to ops with specific field error detail Retry Logic: Scheduled batch retry every 6 hours for failed uploads; max 5 retries within T+3 window; escalation to Compliance Officer if T+3 breach imminent Security: Mutual TLS; digital signature on upload payload; field-level encryption for PAN and Aadhaar reference before transmission Data Masking: Upload payload encrypted end-to-end; audit log records upload attempt and result only — no raw PII stored in log files

Artifact F — Sample User Story with Given-When-Then Acceptance Criteria (US-01)
US-01 — Save Draft Claim | Policyholder | Must | 8 Story Points
"As a policyholder, I want to save my claim as a draft, so that I can complete it later without losing the information I've already entered."
Acceptance Criteria — Given / When / Then
AC-01 Given the user has filled in the minimum required fields (claim type, incident date, policy number) — When the user clicks "Save Draft" — Then the system stores the claim with "Draft" status and displays a confirmation message with a draft reference ID
AC-02 Given a saved draft exists for the user — When the user logs back into the portal — Then the draft claim is visible on their dashboard with a "Resume Draft" call-to-action and the last-edited timestamp displayed
AC-03 Given a draft has been saved — When the user resumes and makes changes — Then the system overwrites the previous draft (not creates a new one) and updates the last-modified timestamp
AC-04 Given a draft is older than 30 days — When the user views their dashboard — Then the draft is highlighted with an expiry warning; draft is auto-deleted at 45 days with a 7-day prior email alert
AC-05 Given the user is on a mobile device with a poor connection — When the session times out mid-form — Then the system auto-saves the last valid state as a draft and shows a recovery notification on next login

"As a policyholder, I want to view my claim status in real-time, so that I never need to call support to find out where my claim stands."

AC-02 Given a claim status changes in the backend system — When the change is processed — Then the portal reflects the updated status within 60 seconds without requiring a manual page refresh

Key Learnings

What these engagements taught me about effective Business Analysis — lessons that go beyond methodology and into the mindset of good BA practice.

💡
Visibility Reduces Friction
When teams can see project status in real-time, communication shifts from reactive to proactive. Transparency is a product feature — designing it in intentionally saves dozens of status-check conversations every week.
🎯
Standardization Creates Shared Language
Inconsistent definitions are not just inefficient — they erode trust. A unified KPI data dictionary doesn't just save time on reporting; it eliminates the low-level organizational conflict that comes from different teams "winning" their interpretation of a metric.
📊
KPIs Must Drive Actions
The right question to ask when defining a KPI: "what decision would you make if this number changed?" If there's no clear answer, it's a vanity metric. Good BA means being willing to push back on the metrics stakeholders think they want.
👥
Alignment Early Saves Rework Late
Stakeholder misalignment is the #1 cause of delivery failure. Running a proper elicitation session in week 1 — even when it feels slow — prevents the far more expensive experience of rebuilding a solution that solves the wrong problem.
🚀
Adoption Is the Real Success Metric
A technically excellent solution that nobody uses is a failed BA engagement. Designing for adoption — keeping intake forms under 2 minutes, making dashboards load fast, building for how people actually work — separates good analysis from great analysis.
🔍
Root Causes Over Symptoms
5-Why analysis revealed that "deadline misses" were not a motivation problem — they were a visibility and structure problem. Treating the symptom (reminders, pressure) would have failed. Treating the root cause (no designed workflow) created lasting change.
Guiding Principle
"Focus on solving business problems — not just documenting requirements."

Documentation is an output of good analysis — not the goal itself. Every requirement, every user story, every process map exists in service of a business outcome that matters to a real person.
